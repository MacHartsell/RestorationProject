Joseph Smith: A Brief History

First Prophet of the Modern Era

Joseph Smith was called by God to be the first prophet of the modern era. Throughout his adult life, he received revelations and guidance as to how Church members should be organized and supported - just like the prophets of the Bible.

Translator of the Book of Mormon

Joseph, along with a scribe, translated the Book of Mormon from Reformed Egyptian into English. He then directed the printing of the book, putting another Testament of Jesus Christ into the hands of anyone who desired to come closer to Christ.

We read about Joseph's call to translate in Doctrine and Covenants Section 5 verse 4:

"And you have a gift to translate the plates; and this is the first gift that I bestowed upon you; and I have commanded that you should pretend to no other gift until my purpose is fulfilled in this; for I will grant unto you no other gift until it is finished."

Read more of this section here: Section 5 of the Doctrine and Covenants

Revelator and Producer

One scholarly article writes:

"The revelations and translations of Joseph Smith have made him known worldwide. Since the time these texts were recorded, they have been revered as God’s word and dismissed as frauds, considered canonical and regarded as blasphemous. Reflecting in 1841 on Smith’s production of such texts, one writer noted that 'it is difficult to imagine a more difficult literary task than to write what may be termed a continuation of the Scriptures.' But producing scripture, Joseph Smith believed, was a fundamental component of his role as a revelator and translator..."

(Jensen, Robin Scott, Robert J. Woodford, and Steven C. Harper, eds. “Joseph Smith as Revelator and Translator” In The Joseph Smith Papers, Revelations and Translation, Volume 1: Manuscript Revelation Books, ixx–xxvi. Salt Lake City: Church Historian’s Press, 2011.)

A martyr for the Church and for God

While being held up in jail, Joseph received a visit from around 200 angry mob members. These men invaded the jail and killed Joseph and his brother, Hyrum.

The deaths of these two men left Saints everywhere mourning. Poet Eliza R. Snow wrote the following shortly after the death of Joseph and Hyrum:

Now Zion mourns—she mourns an earthly head:
The Prophet and the Patriarch are dead!
The blackest deed that men or devils know
Since Calv’ry’s scene, has laid the brothers low!
One in their life, and one in death—they prov’d,
How strong their friendship—how they truly lov’d:
True to their mission, until death, they stood,
Then seal’d their testimony with their blood.
