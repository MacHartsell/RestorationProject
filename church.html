Other Primary Accounts of the First Vision

  1. Home

Why is there more than one account of the First Vision?

Put simply, Joseph had the chance to recount the First Vision many times throughout his life. Because it was such a momentous life experience for him, and because this moment was foundational to the restoration of Christ's true church, Joseph recorded this vision at a few crucial points during his time as Prophet. While there are differences between the accounts, this does not automatically invalidate his experience; rather, the different points Joseph makes during each account highlight what was most significant to him at that time.

Let's take a look at the 3 other accounts not mentioned in the First Vision Details page of this site.

1832 Account

Read the account here: 1832 Account

The 1832 account is the earliest known primary account.

  • The 1832 account is the only account written by Joseph Smith himself.
  • It focuses more on the desire that Joseph had to be forgiven of his sins, in addition to knowing which church was true.
  • Distinct from other accounts, the 1832 publication states that "the Lord" appeared to Joseph, instead of two personages.

1835 Account

Read the account here: 1835 Account

The 1835 account is a narration of the event to a man named Robert Mathews, who was visiting Joseph's town.

  • The 1835 account was recorded in Joseph's journal by a scribe.
  • The key themes of this retelling are the struggle to find the true church, feelings of opposition as he prayed, and angels that supported him and were present in the moment.
  • It is the only account to mention angels.

1842 Account

Read the account here: 1842 Account

The 1842 account was written for John Wentworth, the editor of the Chicago Democrat. Head of one fo the most famous newspapers of the day, Wentworth requested information about the Latter-Day Saints to publish.

  • The 1842 account clarifies that Joseph saw 2 seperate personages, being God and Jesus Christ.
  • Joseph highlights the confusion that he felt stirring in his soul because of the many religions of the day.
  • This account was paired with the Articles of Faith, a summarization of what members of the Church believe - which was also published by Wentworth.
